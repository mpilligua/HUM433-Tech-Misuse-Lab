Apple AirTag

A small Bluetooth tracker that helps people find lost belongings like keys or bags. It's coin-sized, sticks to or slips inside almost anything, and reports its location through a network of nearby Apple devices.

Strategy: Scenario Analysis

Most product reviews ask what a product does. This one asks who's around it. You imagine real situations - people, tensions, relationships - and check whether the product changes what's possible in those situations. Misuse rarely shows up in the spec sheet. It shows up in the scenario.

Motive
A reason to misuse it.
Access
Proximity to the target.
Opportunity
What the product newly enables.

When all three line up, a product becomes a misuse vector.

Pick a scenario to investigate

Three situations. Which would you investigate first for possible misuse?

A couple recently ended their relationship and are still exchanging belongings.
A parent in a custody dispute wants to know where their child goes after school.
A journalist meets a confidential source in a public location.

Identify motive, access, and opportunity in this scene.
Motive

Access
They can use the product, feature, controls or physical device.
Opportunity
The product gives them a capability that makes the misuse possible or easier.
All three legs are present. Motive, access, and opportunity all line up. This is what a misuse vector looks like.

That was scenario-based misuse analysis.

You watched a situation and checked it against the same lens. When you're designing a new product - or evaluating one - don't start with the features. Start with the people around it, and ask: motive, access, opportunity. If all three line up somewhere, you've found a misuse vector before it finds you.
